﻿The computer company Apple has always been lauded for it’s seer like ability to predict the next big thing in consumer tech and then release a killer product, catching the competition with their pants down. It was rumored that if Steve Jobs hadn’t died of pancreatic cancer, he would have steered Apple into the automotive industry. As a consumer, the IoT has just gotten a start in the automobile. Since smart phones have gained massive popularity over the last decade, it seems natural that your mobile phone should interface with your main means of mobility- your car. There is a push to develop mobile apps that interface with the car’s onboard computer. Ford has published an API for interacting with its onboard computer to then control your smart phone - think being able to call a friend, play the next track on your internet radio, or ask for directions. Likewise, BMW has released a proprietary app which hints at IoT-ish functionality as it allows BMW owners with an iPhone or Android device to remotely unlock their car, start the climate control system, locate their car using GPS, and push navigation routes to their car. This is all accomplished over the mobile network and is only available on select models.

Automobiles connecting to mobile networks is not a new concept. Ford’s API allows programmers to tap into Ford’s OnStar system which Ford has used for years to provide roadside assistance to drivers covered by a mobile network. A European Union act was passed earlier this year stipulating that all new cars must be fitted with a GPS and GSM receiver. This is similar to the OnStar system in that its current purpose is to assist malfunctioning vehicles and definitely not let shady NSA agents track your every step. Privacy concerns aside, the hope is that in the future, IoT-like applications will be able to piggyback these sorts of wireless systems and give automobiles IoT functionality.

We can see that true IoT-like functionality doesn’t currently exist in the automotive industry. To truly be part of the IoT, we would expect a car to act like an independent node, communicating autonomously with its internet-embedded surroundings such that it could learn from objects like nearby cars, businesses, road surfaces, and people to do more sophisticated things than just switching the song on your Pandora radio. Zigbee represents just one of the emerging 802.15.4 wireless standards whose primary purpose is low power, short range communication- perfect for a network of ordinary domestic objects. Named after the dance that bees do to communicate the whereabouts of flower fields, Zigbee’s goal is to provide communication between a mesh of domestic devices. Zigbee was first designed in the late 90s, however businesses have taken almost ten years to standardize Zigbee protocols and band together to start creating devices which can communicate with different Zigbee devices. There are currently over 100 companies which have agreed to produce their proprietary wireless products using this common protocol, which is good because before any networking technology can gain footing, it needs a critical mass of producers and users.

There are already many devices that have been fitted with Zigbee communicators. Their website lists things like wireless light bulbs, sockets, switches, dimmers, locks, access keys, and motion detectors; all boring domestic widgets. Who really cares? The cool aspect of Zigbee isn’t necessarily the things that it connects, but how they connect and convey useful information. Zigbee devices create a mesh network much like the internet. A communication between two devices might take place between a series of hops on other Zigbee devices. Thus Zigbee devices serve to both send original data and forward data. So, even though two devices have a maximum range of 100m, a network could potentially expand to your entire 9-story mansion. Indeed, this is how one hotel in Las Vegas controls its room environment and entertainment system. Each room is fitted with several Zigbee sensors and controllers. A base station connects this system to the larger internet so that hotel management can have absolute control. However, most of the time this is not needed for the Zigbee network can locally detect when a room has become vacant and do things like close the blinds, shut the lights off, and turn on the Roomba (well, not quite yet).

Again, Zigbee is not the official local mesh network standard. There are other competitors such as Zwave. We will probably see something similar to HDDVD and BluRay or Betamax and VHS occur where one new consumer technology eventually wins out. We will benefit as consumers from this decision because it will lead to more connected devices in our homes, working together to make smart decisions on how to best cater to us and save the planet’s resources

There are test technologies being developed which will probably find homes in a myriad array of devices which will be connected to the IoT. This past September, Intel announced the ‘Quark’ processor. It is ultra-low power consuming yet powerful enough to run applications which require a full network stack. In their announcement, Intel specifically stated that this technology is ment for small devices connecting to the internet of things. Large corporations are taking the IoT seriously. Improvements in sensor technology is also a requirement for a functioning IoT. MEMS or microelectromechanical systems are composites of tiny sensors and microprocessor. They use non-classical physics techniques to accurately gather and process data like acceleration, gyroscopic information, and can even harvest energy from the environment to power themselves. MEMS is beginning to become mainstream especially in the auto industry where they are used in sensors which deploy airbags and sense tire pressure. MEMS is also used within the Oculus Rift, the virtual reality goggles which is trying to revolutionize gaming. It uses MEMS to predict the motion of a user’s head so that it can display the relevant frames to the user with low latency.

﻿ The internet of things will mean that your actions and reactions are more connected to the internet than ever before. With the benefits this brings there are also major security and privacy issues introduced. Privacy involves the right to keep your information private and control who has access it to it, poor security will undoubtedly put a users privacy at risk. Many objects within the internet of things track movement of the user or the users daily actions without any obvious signal that the information is being recorded. This means that the user can be creating data about their actions unknowingly. Regardless of source data needs to be kept privately and stored securely, to achieve this a combined effort between the company gathering data and the governments is implemented.

﻿ Privacy is also important on a much larger scale, with more and more systems in the public and industrial domain connected to the internet, the security of these needs to be a priority. Consider smart grids, a country's power controlled by computational devices, if hackers could infiltrate these the effects could be severe. The same applies to allowing the automation of many procedures, especially when these are also connected to the internet. On a personal level especially with medical devices security is a concern. Currently people have medical devices within them or linked to them that are also connected to wirelessly to the internet. These devices can often perform actions that could be fatal, for example a pacemaker delivering a shock to a heart in a normal rhythm, or an insulin pump giving a fatal dose. The video opposite shows a famous example of a hacker attacking one of these devices.

﻿ There are a number of policies in used to help keep data private especially within the IoT. However these policies need to be enforced so clients privacy can be guaranteed. To tackle privacy issues, self regulation or legislative regulation can be used. Whilst self-regulation is less costly publicly, its effectiveness is questioned as participation is not enforced and companies can use policies to their own advantage. Legislative regulation is costly and hard to enforce, however allows direct dictation of guidelines to be followed. A compromise is a co-legislation approach, where the government outlines a framework for companies to follow and they implement it with there own individual policies and joint enforcement.
